Exercise 3: Distinguish Verb Pairs
Analyze Subtle Distinctions in Tactical Effect
For each scenario, two similar verbs are offered. Select the correct one based on the precise ATP-112 definitions. Pay attention to the subtle distinctions in tactical effect.
Pair: SECURE vs SEIZE
"Your unit must gain control of an ammunition depot to prevent its destruction by retreating enemy forces. The depot is currently unoccupied but has been rigged with explosives."
SECURE — prevent use/destruction SEIZE — take by force
Pair: BLOCK vs DENY vs FIX
"Your unit must prevent the enemy reserve force from moving south along ROUTE TANGO to reinforce their positions. You are to engage them if they attempt to move, but your main purpose is to stop movement along this specific route."
BLOCK — stop movement along a route DENY — prevent use of an area FIX — prevent all movement
Pair: CONTAIN vs ISOLATE
"Your unit must prevent the enemy platoon in the northern sector from withdrawing to join their main body. You do not need to destroy them — only ensure they cannot leave their current area of operations."
CONTAIN — stop withdrawal/maneuver ISOLATE — separate from support
Pair: DISRUPT vs DELAY
"Your unit must slow the enemy advance through the valley without becoming decisively engaged, trading space for time over a 6-hour period while the main body consolidates."
DISRUPT — break up temporarily DELAY — slow advance, trade space for time
Pair: DEFEAT vs DESTROY
"Your battalion must engage the enemy brigade in the southern sector and make them unable to continue resisting — this may involve destroying key units, forcing their withdrawal, or breaking their cohesion."
DEFEAT — render unable/unwilling to resist DESTROY — render physically incapable of function

Reference section based on ATP-112 (2020) and STANAG 2014 Ed9. Use these rules when writing or evaluating operational orders and mission statements. All task verbs must be selected based on tactical effect, not translated directly from another language.
Mission Statement — Formula & Checklist
ATP-112 / STANAG 2014
MISSION STATEMENT FORMULA: [UNIT] is to / are to [TASK VERB] [OBJECT + LOCATION] [TIMING — when applicable] IOT [PURPOSE / DESIRED EFFECT] EXAMPLE: C COY is to DENY EAGLE BRIDGE NLT H+1 IOT set conditions for the denial of INS LLoC through GZ. —————————————————————————————— CHECKLIST — all 5 elements must be present: ✓ WHO — the unit assigned the task ✓ WHAT — the ATP-112 TASK VERB (in capitals) ✓ WHERE — the object and its location ✓ WHEN — timing (may be implied by H-Hour) ✓ WHY — IOT + purpose/effect HARD RULES: • One sentence only — never two • One task verb only • No "and" connecting two tasks • Task verb in CAPITALS • IOT connects task to purpose • Obligation: is to / are to (not should/could/shall)
Mission Statement — ATP-112 / STANAG 2014
The IOT clause is not optional. A mission without a purpose is incomplete. The purpose explains why the task matters — it gives subordinates the context to act independently if the situation changes.

Verb Selection — Choosing the Correct Task Verb
ATP-112 Protocol
VERB SELECTION PROCESS: Step 1: Identify the TACTICAL EFFECT required. • Do you need to take terrain? → SEIZE • Do you need to protect terrain? → SECURE • Do you need to eliminate all enemy? → CLEAR • Do you need to stop movement on a route? → BLOCK • Do you need to prevent use of an area? → DENY • Do you need permanent physical damage? → DESTROY • Do you need temporary incapacity? → NEUTRALIZE • Do you need to break enemy will? → DEFEAT Step 2: Check the PRECISE ATP-112 definition. Never assume. Verify. Similar verbs have distinct legal/tactical meanings. Step 3: If no NATO verb matches the effect, use CONDUCT + [activity name]. —————————————————————————————— COMMON ERRORS: ✗ Translating directly from another language (Spanish "tomar" → "take" ✗ → SEIZE ✓) ✗ Using a generic verb when a precise one exists ("get control of" ✗ → SEIZE or SECURE ✓) ✗ Confusing temporary vs permanent effects (DESTROY for a 2-hour suppression ✗ → NEUTRALIZE ✓) ✗ Using CONDUCT when a NATO verb exists (CONDUCT SEIZURE ✗ → SEIZE ✓)
Verb Selection Protocol — ATP-112
The most frequent error in NATO order-writing examinations is verb imprecision. An examiner will mark down any use of a non-NATO verb when an ATP-112 verb exists, or the use of an ATP-112 verb that does not match the described tactical effect.

Verb Differentiation — Critical Pairs Quick Reference
6 critical pairs
CRITICAL DISTINCTIONS — MEMORIZE THESE: DEFEAT vs DESTROY DEFEAT = enemy unable/unwilling to resist (broad) DESTROY = target physically incapable of function (narrow) → "DEFEAT the enemy force; DESTROY the radar installation." SECURE vs SEIZE SECURE = gain control to PREVENT use or destruction SEIZE = take possession BY FORCE from enemy control → "SECURE the unoccupied depot; SEIZE the defended bridge." BLOCK vs DENY vs FIX BLOCK = stop movement along a SPECIFIC ROUTE DENY = prevent USE of an area or facility (broader) FIX = prevent enemy from MOVING AT ALL from position → "BLOCK Route Tango; DENY the bridge; FIX the enemy platoon." NEUTRALIZE vs DISRUPT NEUTRALIZE = temporarily INCAPABLE (hours, complete) DISRUPT = temporarily INEFFECTIVE (minutes, partial) → "NEUTRALIZE air defenses for the assault; DISRUPT reinforcements." CONTAIN vs ISOLATE CONTAIN = stop WITHDRAWAL or MANEUVER beyond area ISOLATE = SEPARATE from support (supplies, reinforcements) → "CONTAIN them in the pocket; ISOLATE them from their logistics." SCREEN vs GUARD vs COVER SCREEN = EARLY WARNING only — observe, report GUARD = PROTECT against attack — can engage COVER = stand BETWEEN enemy and protected force → "SCREEN the flank; GUARD the convoy; COVER the withdrawal."
Verb Differentiation — ATP-112 Definitions
These six pairs represent the most commonly confused verbs in NATO orders. In an examination, you will be tested on at least three of these distinctions. Learn the memory aids.

CONDUCT + Activity & Common Errors
Rules & corrections
WHEN TO USE CONDUCT: Use CONDUCT + [activity name] when: ✓ No ATP-112 task verb describes the tactical effect ✓ The operation is non-kinetic (info ops, CIMIC, etc.) ✓ The operation is a specialized activity (SAR, EOD) ✓ The activity combines multiple tactical effects without a single covering verb CORRECT USAGE: • CONDUCT INFO OPS — no NATO verb for information ops • CONDUCT SAR — specialized search and rescue • CONDUCT CIMIC TASKS — civil-military cooperation • CONDUCT CORDON — combines CONTAIN + SCREEN • CONDUCT RECONNAISSANCE — broader than LOCATE/SCREEN INCORRECT USAGE: ✗ CONDUCT SEIZURE — SEIZE exists as a NATO verb ✗ CONDUCT DESTRUCTION — DESTROY exists ✗ CONDUCT ATTACK — ATTACK exists ✗ CONDUCT DEFENCE — DEFEND exists (though less common) —————————————————————————————— RULE OF THUMB: If an ATP-112 verb exists → use it. If no ATP-112 verb exists → CONDUCT + activity. Never use CONDUCT as a "lazy" substitute for a verb you can't remember.
CONDUCT + Activity — ATP-112 Supplementary Guidance
CONDUCT is not a shortcut. Using CONDUCT when a NATO verb exists signals to the examiner that you do not know the correct ATP-112 verb. Reserve CONDUCT for operations that genuinely lack a NATO task verb.
Common Errors Before & After — Mission Statement Corrections
✗ Direct translation from Spanish — wrong verb
"B Coy is to TAKE Eagle Bridge to prevent enemy escape."
"Take" is not an ATP-112 task verb. The tactical effect is SEIZE (take by force).
✓ Correct — ATP-112 verb based on effect
"B COY is to SEIZE EAGLE BRIDGE IOT prevent En escape."
SEIZE = take possession by force. Correct NATO verb.
✗ Wrong effect — temporary action, permanent verb
"Fire support is to DESTROY En artillery for the duration of the assault."
DESTROY = permanent. The intent is temporary (duration of assault).
✓ Correct — temporary effect = NEUTRALIZE
"Fire support is to NEUTRALIZE En artillery for the duration of the assault."
NEUTRALIZE = temporarily incapable. Matches the tactical effect.
✗ Using CONDUCT when NATO verb exists
"A Coy is to CONDUCT SEIZURE of Objective Hawk IOT allow FF advance."
SEIZE is a valid ATP-112 verb. CONDUCT is unnecessary and incorrect.
✓ Correct — direct NATO verb
"A COY is to SEIZE OBJECTIVE HAWK IOT allow FF advance."
Clean, precise, and doctrinally correct.
✗ Two tasks in one mission statement
"C Coy is to SEIZE the bridge and HOLD it against counter-attack."
One mission = one task verb. HOLD belongs in Execution as a BPT task.
✓ Correct — one task; second in Execution
"C COY is to SEIZE THE BRIDGE IOT deny En crossing.
[In Execution: On SEIZING THE BRIDGE, C COY is to CONSOLIDATE and BPT HOLD against counter-attack.]"
